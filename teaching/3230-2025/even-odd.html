Even and Odd Permutations

This is a short note to describe explicitly which elements of \(S_4\) (the symmetric group on \(4\) elements) and \(D_4\) (the symmetries of a square) are even and which are odd. Recall that we can write every permutation \(\sigma\) as a product of transpositions; then \(\sigma\) is even if it is the product of an even number of transpositions, and odd if it is the product of an odd number of transpositions.

\(D_4\)

Let’s number the vertices of our square as \(1\), \(2\), \(3\), and \(4\) (in order as we go around the square clockwise). In class we described the symmetries explicitly: There are \(4\) rotations of the square, which we can write as permutations \[e, \qquad % \readlist\thecycle{1,2,3,4}% (\foreachitem\i\in\thecycle{\ifnum\icnt=1\else\,\fi\i})% , \qquad % \readlist\thecycle{1,3}% (\foreachitem\i\in\thecycle{\ifnum\icnt=1\else\,\fi\i})% % \readlist\thecycle{2,4}% (\foreachitem\i\in\thecycle{\ifnum\icnt=1\else\,\fi\i})% , \qquad % \readlist\thecycle{1,4,3,4}% (\foreachitem\i\in\thecycle{\ifnum\icnt=1\else\,\fi\i})%\] There are \(2\) flips across the horizontal and vertical axes: \[% \readlist\thecycle{1,2}% (\foreachitem\i\in\thecycle{\ifnum\icnt=1\else\,\fi\i})% % \readlist\thecycle{3,4}% (\foreachitem\i\in\thecycle{\ifnum\icnt=1\else\,\fi\i})% , \qquad % \readlist\thecycle{1,4}% (\foreachitem\i\in\thecycle{\ifnum\icnt=1\else\,\fi\i})% % \readlist\thecycle{2,3}% (\foreachitem\i\in\thecycle{\ifnum\icnt=1\else\,\fi\i})%\] And there are \(2\) flips across the diagonal axes: \[% \readlist\thecycle{1,3}% (\foreachitem\i\in\thecycle{\ifnum\icnt=1\else\,\fi\i})% , \qquad% \readlist\thecycle{2,4}% (\foreachitem\i\in\thecycle{\ifnum\icnt=1\else\,\fi\i})%\]

Thus, there are \(4\) even permutations, namely \[e,\qquad % \readlist\thecycle{1,3}% (\foreachitem\i\in\thecycle{\ifnum\icnt=1\else\,\fi\i})% % \readlist\thecycle{2,4}% (\foreachitem\i\in\thecycle{\ifnum\icnt=1\else\,\fi\i})% , \qquad % \readlist\thecycle{1,2}% (\foreachitem\i\in\thecycle{\ifnum\icnt=1\else\,\fi\i})% % \readlist\thecycle{3,4}% (\foreachitem\i\in\thecycle{\ifnum\icnt=1\else\,\fi\i})% , \qquad % \readlist\thecycle{1,4}% (\foreachitem\i\in\thecycle{\ifnum\icnt=1\else\,\fi\i})% % \readlist\thecycle{2,3}% (\foreachitem\i\in\thecycle{\ifnum\icnt=1\else\,\fi\i})%\] and there are \(4\) odd permutations, namely \[% \readlist\thecycle{1,2,3,4}% (\foreachitem\i\in\thecycle{\ifnum\icnt=1\else\,\fi\i})% , \qquad % \readlist\thecycle{1,4,3,4}% (\foreachitem\i\in\thecycle{\ifnum\icnt=1\else\,\fi\i})% , \qquad % \readlist\thecycle{1,3}% (\foreachitem\i\in\thecycle{\ifnum\icnt=1\else\,\fi\i})% , \qquad% \readlist\thecycle{2,4}% (\foreachitem\i\in\thecycle{\ifnum\icnt=1\else\,\fi\i})%\] Recall that a \(k\)-cycle is the product of \(k-1\) transpositions, so these length \(4\)-cycles are the products of \(3\) transpositions, so they are odd.

We see that half of the elements of \(D_4\) are even and half are odd.

\(S_4\)

The symmetric group \(S_4\) has \(4!=24\) permutations, and we saw in class that half of them are even and half are odd. So we are looking for \(12\) even permutations (the elements of \(A_4\)) and \(12\) odd permutations.

As we saw in lecture, the identity element \(e\) is an even permutation.

Now we count transpositions (which will be odd): The data of a transposition consists exactly of a choice of \(2\) elements from \(\{1, 2, 3, 4\}\), namely, which two elements are being swapped. So there are \(\binom{4}{2}=6\) transpositions, namely \[% \readlist\thecycle{1,2}% (\foreachitem\i\in\thecycle{\ifnum\icnt=1\else\,\fi\i})% ,\quad % \readlist\thecycle{1,3}% (\foreachitem\i\in\thecycle{\ifnum\icnt=1\else\,\fi\i})% ,\quad % \readlist\thecycle{1,4}% (\foreachitem\i\in\thecycle{\ifnum\icnt=1\else\,\fi\i})% ,\quad % \readlist\thecycle{2,3}% (\foreachitem\i\in\thecycle{\ifnum\icnt=1\else\,\fi\i})% ,\quad % \readlist\thecycle{2,4}% (\foreachitem\i\in\thecycle{\ifnum\icnt=1\else\,\fi\i})% ,\quad % \readlist\thecycle{3,4}% (\foreachitem\i\in\thecycle{\ifnum\icnt=1\else\,\fi\i})%\]

Now we count products of \(2\) distinct transpositions (which will be even permutations). There are \(2\) kinds of such products: the transpositions can be disjoint, or they can have an element in common. In the first case, one transposition will swap the element \(1\) with something, and one will not. Moreover, once we’ve chosen our first transposition, the second simply swaps the \(2\) elements remaining. So we have \(3\) such products: \[% \readlist\thecycle{1,2}% (\foreachitem\i\in\thecycle{\ifnum\icnt=1\else\,\fi\i})% % \readlist\thecycle{3,4}% (\foreachitem\i\in\thecycle{\ifnum\icnt=1\else\,\fi\i})% ,\quad % \readlist\thecycle{1,3}% (\foreachitem\i\in\thecycle{\ifnum\icnt=1\else\,\fi\i})% % \readlist\thecycle{2,4}% (\foreachitem\i\in\thecycle{\ifnum\icnt=1\else\,\fi\i})% ,\quad % \readlist\thecycle{1,4}% (\foreachitem\i\in\thecycle{\ifnum\icnt=1\else\,\fi\i})% % \readlist\thecycle{2,3}% (\foreachitem\i\in\thecycle{\ifnum\icnt=1\else\,\fi\i})%\] In the second case, we observe that the product \(% \readlist\thecycle{a,b}% (\foreachitem\i\in\thecycle{\ifnum\icnt=1\else\,\fi\i})% % \readlist\thecycle{a,c}% (\foreachitem\i\in\thecycle{\ifnum\icnt=1\else\,\fi\i})%\) is the \(3\)-cycle \(% \readlist\thecycle{a,c,b}% (\foreachitem\i\in\thecycle{\ifnum\icnt=1\else\,\fi\i})%\), so it’s enough to count permutations represented by \(3\)-cycles. If a \(3\)-cycle contains \(1\), it has the form \(% \readlist\thecycle{1,a,b}% (\foreachitem\i\in\thecycle{\ifnum\icnt=1\else\,\fi\i})%\), and there are \(3\) choices for \(a\) and then \(2\) choices for \(b\). So this gives us \(6\) permutations: \[% \readlist\thecycle{1,2,3}% (\foreachitem\i\in\thecycle{\ifnum\icnt=1\else\,\fi\i})% ,\quad % \readlist\thecycle{1,2,4}% (\foreachitem\i\in\thecycle{\ifnum\icnt=1\else\,\fi\i})% ,\quad % \readlist\thecycle{1,3,2}% (\foreachitem\i\in\thecycle{\ifnum\icnt=1\else\,\fi\i})% ,\quad % \readlist\thecycle{1,3,4}% (\foreachitem\i\in\thecycle{\ifnum\icnt=1\else\,\fi\i})% , \quad % \readlist\thecycle{1,4,2}% (\foreachitem\i\in\thecycle{\ifnum\icnt=1\else\,\fi\i})% , \quad % \readlist\thecycle{1,4,3}% (\foreachitem\i\in\thecycle{\ifnum\icnt=1\else\,\fi\i})%\] If a \(3\)-cycle in \(S_4\) does not contain \(1\), it contains all of \(2, 3, 4\), and there are \(2\) permutations we can make from them: \[% \readlist\thecycle{2,3,4}% (\foreachitem\i\in\thecycle{\ifnum\icnt=1\else\,\fi\i})% , \quad % \readlist\thecycle{2,4,3}% (\foreachitem\i\in\thecycle{\ifnum\icnt=1\else\,\fi\i})%\] Check for yourself that all of these \(3\)-cycles represent different permutations! But note that (for example) \(% \readlist\thecycle{1,2,3}% (\foreachitem\i\in\thecycle{\ifnum\icnt=1\else\,\fi\i})%\) and \(% \readlist\thecycle{2,3,1}% (\foreachitem\i\in\thecycle{\ifnum\icnt=1\else\,\fi\i})%\) represent the same permutation, so these really are all of the permutations in \(S_4\) represented by \(3\)-cycles.

There are \(6\) elements of \(S_4\) left to find, and we will see they are all \(4\)-cycles; since \(4\)-cycles are products of \(3\) transpositions, they are odd. Now we can write any length-\(4\) cycle in the form \(% \readlist\thecycle{1,a,b,c}% (\foreachitem\i\in\thecycle{\ifnum\icnt=1\else\,\fi\i})%\), where \(\{a,b,c\}=\{2,3,4\}\). Thus, writing down a \(4\)-cycle amounts to choosing an ordering for the elements of \(\{2,3,4\}\), and there are \(6\) orderings. Explicitly, we have \[% \readlist\thecycle{1,2,3,4}% (\foreachitem\i\in\thecycle{\ifnum\icnt=1\else\,\fi\i})% ,\quad % \readlist\thecycle{1,2,4,3}% (\foreachitem\i\in\thecycle{\ifnum\icnt=1\else\,\fi\i})% , \quad% \readlist\thecycle{1,3,2,4}% (\foreachitem\i\in\thecycle{\ifnum\icnt=1\else\,\fi\i})% , \quad % \readlist\thecycle{1,3,4,2}% (\foreachitem\i\in\thecycle{\ifnum\icnt=1\else\,\fi\i})% , \quad % \readlist\thecycle{1,4,2,3}% (\foreachitem\i\in\thecycle{\ifnum\icnt=1\else\,\fi\i})% , \quad % \readlist\thecycle{1,4,3,2}% (\foreachitem\i\in\thecycle{\ifnum\icnt=1\else\,\fi\i})%\] Again, check for yourself explicitly that these are all different permutations.

We have found \(24\) elements of \(S_4\), so we have listed every element. Counting up the even and odd permutations above, we see that we have \(12\) even permutations and \(12\) odd permutations, which is exactly what our theorem from class said should happen.
